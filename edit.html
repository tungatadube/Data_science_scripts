  2. Allow someone to close the switch

  4. Increase the current before closing the switch again
  5. Observe the magnitude of the motion of the conductor
  6. Reverse the direction of the current (interchange the positive or negative terminals of your supply.) before closing the switch
  7. Observe the direction of the motion of the wire
  8. Use two magnets stacked one on top of the other before closing the switch.
  9. Observe the magnitude of motion of the wire
  10. Reverse the poles of the magnet
  11. Observe the direction of motion of the wire
  12. Change the angle of the current carrying conductor in the magnetic field.
  13. Observe the magnitude of the motion of the conductor.
Expected Observations:
  • Your investigation should show that:
  • The force moving the conductor is increased by ,
  • Increasing the current
  • Using a stronger magnet
  • The direction of the force is reversed if;
  • The direction of the current is reversed
  • The direction of the magnetic field is reversed
  • The force depends on the angle between the wire and the magnetic field. The force is,
  • Greatest when the wire is perpendicular to the magnetic field
  • Zero when the wire is parallel to the magnetic field lines
  • The direction of the force is always at right angles to the wire and the field lines.
  • The direction of the force can be worked out from the direction of the field lines and the current using a rule known as Fleming’s left hand rule.
Conclusion:
  • There is a force acting on a current carrying conductor when it is placed in a magnetic field.
  • The direction of this force is given by using Fleming’s left hand detailed below

The solenoid

CONTENT TO BE TYPED

  • A solenoid is just a current carrying conductor wound in such a shape that it will behave like a magnet with a north and south pole.
  • To identify the poles, look into the solenoid from one end. If the current is moving in a clockwiSe direction then that face will behave like a South pole.
  • If however the curent moves in an aNticlockwise direction then that particular end will behave like a North pole.

Application of the magnetic field of a current

  • We apply the magnetic effect of a current to many everyday instances. The motion of the current carrying conductor allows electric bells to produce sound, motors to move and a host of other everyday examples.
  • We look at the workings of an electric bell and the motor.

The Electric bell

  • Having understood how a magnetic field is developed around a solenoid we delve into a simple application of a solenoid system, the electric bell. An electric bell is shown in Fig 10.2.7 below.



  • Closing switch S, causes a current to flow from E.
  • When current flows, the U shaped solenoid is induced with a magnetic field, causing its iron core to be magnetic hence attracting the iron arm A to move upwards about the pivot P.
  • When the arm moves upwards it strikes the bell B, disconecting the circuit at T.
  • Current ceases to flow in the solenoid making the magnetic field to disappear.
  • The spring arm T then falls back , when it completes the circuit it is pulled up again striking the bell again.
  • This occurs many times in one second thereby causing a continuous sound to be heard.

The d.c motor




  • A d.c motor is a device that converts direct current (electrical energy) into mechanical energy.
  • In order to understand the operating principle of d.c motor, we need to first look into its constructional features.
  • The split ring allows the direction of current to change depending on the position of the arm in the magnetic field
  • The permanent magnets introduce a magnetic field that will interact with the magnetic field from the current carrying coil.
  • Fleming’s left hand rule will give the direction of the motion of the coil in the magnetic field.
  • In the arm dc the current flows from d to c, applying Fleming’s left hand rule to this arm will show that it is under a force that causes it to move down wards.
  • Similarly the arm ba has a current that flows from b to a, applying Fleming’s left hand rule will show that the arm has a motion that moves it upwards.
  • The totality of these two motions will combine to cause the coil to rotate about its fixed axis in a clockwise sense in this case.
  • Reversing the direction of the current or the magnetic field will cause the motion to go anticlockwise. (You may have seen hand drills that have a switch to reverse the motion of the drill-bit).
